Introduction to Networking

Computer networking is the backbone of the digital world. At its simplest, a network consists of two or more computers connected together so they can share data, resources (like printers or internet connections), and communicate.

1. The Core Components

Every network requires a mix of hardware to connect devices and direct traffic.

  • End Devices: These are the source and destination of data—laptops, smartphones, servers, and smart TVs.
  • Switches: A switch connects multiple devices together within the same local network (like a local area network, or LAN). It acts like a smart power strip, directing data only to the specific device it was meant for.
  • Routers: A router connects entirely different networks together. Its job is to look at a piece of data's destination and choose the best path for it to travel across the internet to get there.

2. Network Models (The Rules of the Road)

For different devices (like an Apple iPhone and a Dell server) to talk to each other, they have to agree on a strict set of rules called protocols. These rules are organized into layers, most commonly represented by the TCP/IP Model.

When you send a message, it travels from the top layer down to the physical hardware, across the network, and back up the layers on the receiving device.

The Four Layers of TCP/IP

Layer Primary Function Everyday Example / Protocol
Application Where user data originates and interacts with software. HTTP (Websites), DNS (Translates domain names to IP addresses)
Transport Manages the connection between two devices and ensures data arrives intact. TCP (Reliable, checks for errors) / UDP (Fast, used for live video/gaming)
Network Handles routing and logical addressing across different networks. IP (Internet Protocol), Routers
Network Access Moves raw bits over physical mediums (wires, airwaves). Ethernet cables, Wi-Fi, Switches, MAC addresses

3. Essential Network Services

When you type a website name into your browser, two critical background protocols make it work instantly:

DNS (Domain Name System)

The phonebook of the internet. It translates human-friendly names like wikipedia.org into machine-friendly IP addresses like 198.35.26.96.

DHCP (Dynamic Host Configuration Protocol)

The service that automatically hands your phone or laptop an IP address the second you connect to a Wi-Fi network so you don't have to configure it manually.

4. Network Scale: LAN vs. WAN

Networks are generally categorized by the geographic area they cover:

  • LAN (Local Area Network): A network restricted to a small geographic area, like your home, a single office building, or a school. It is fast, private, and highly secure.
  • WAN (Wide Area Network): A network that connects multiple LANs across large geographical distances. The Internet is the largest and most famous example of a WAN, spanning the entire globe.

Threats and Vulnerabilities

Identify and analyze security threats, risks, and vulnerabilities.

What is a Threat?

A threat is any potential occurrence or event—whether malicious, accidental, or environmental—that could cause harm, theft, data loss, or disruption to an organization's assets or operations.

Analogy: A burglar looking for a house to rob, or a severe thunderstorm that might cause a power outage.

What is a Vulnerability?

A vulnerability is a flaw, weakness, or gap in an information system, security procedure, internal control, or implementation that can be exploited by a threat source.

Analogy: A broken lock on the back door of a house, or leaving a window wide open.

Categories and Examples

Common Types of Threats

  • Malicious Threats: Malware (ransomware, spyware), Phishing attacks, DDoS attacks, and insider threats (disgruntled employees).
  • Accidental Threats: Employee error, such as misconfiguring a cloud database or accidentally deleting critical code.
  • Environmental Threats: Natural disasters like earthquakes, floods, fires, or hardware failures due to power surges.

Common Types of Vulnerabilities

  • Software Vulnerabilities: Code bugs like Buffer Overflows, SQL Injection (SQLi), or Cross-Site Scripting (XSS).
  • Human Vulnerabilities: Lack of security awareness, making staff susceptible to social engineering or phishing.
  • Configuration Vulnerabilities: Using default passwords, leaving unneeded ports open, or outdated firmware.

How Threats and Vulnerabilities Interact

A threat cannot harm a system unless a vulnerability exists that it can exploit. Conversely, a vulnerability poses no immediate danger if there is no threat capable of or interested in exploiting it. When a threat successfully exploits a vulnerability, it results in a security incident or breach.

This intersection is mathematically defined in Cybersecurity Risk Management by the following formula:

Risk = Threat × Vulnerability × Asset Value

Without either a threat or a vulnerability, the overall operational risk drops to zero.

Quick Comparison Table

Feature Threat Vulnerability
Origin External or Internal forces (People, Nature, Code) Internal systemic flaws (System design, Controls, People)
Control Cannot be directly controlled or eliminated Can be patched, mitigated, or fixed internally
Nature The actor or action that causes the harm The weakness that allows the harm to happen
Action Required Monitor, Detect, Defend against Identify, Assess, Patch, Mitigate

Defensive Strategies

Organizations must approach threats and vulnerabilities differently to establish a robust security perimeter:

  1. Managing Vulnerabilities: Conduct regular vulnerability scans, perform routine penetration testing, enforce patch management protocols, and deliver continuous security awareness training to staff.
  2. Defending Against Threats: Deploy firewalls, intrusion detection/prevention systems (IDS/IPS), endpoint detection and response (EDR), and maintain immutable backups to bounce back from active attacks.
